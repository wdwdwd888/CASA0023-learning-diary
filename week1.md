# Week 1 – Getting Started with Remote Sensing
This week introduced the basic foundations of remote sensing through both the lecture and the practical. The lecture focused on what remote sensing is, how data are collected, the main observation platforms, key satellite data sources, and some common applications. The practical then extended this by introducing a basic workflow for working with remotely sensed data, including downloading Sentinel and Landsat imagery, exploring image products in QGIS and SNAP, and beginning simple raster processing and comparison.

One of the most useful things I learned this week is that remote sensing is much more than just looking at satellite images. Before this session, I mainly thought of remote sensing as a visual source of information. However, the lecture and practical showed that it is actually a structured process involving sensors, platforms, product types, image resolution, and software-based processing. Even at an introductory level, I had to think about study area selection, cloud cover, date range, and which data source was appropriate. This made me realise that remotely sensed data are shaped by how they are captured and processed, not just by what appears on the screen.

The practical was especially helpful in making these ideas more concrete. By working with Sentinel and Landsat data, I started to understand that different sensors have different strengths and limitations. Sentinel appears useful for finer spatial detail, while Landsat offers a longer-term archive that is valuable for historical comparison. This means that choosing data is not simply a technical step, but something closely linked to the question being asked. I also began to compare the roles of different software environments. QGIS felt more intuitive for opening and visualising imagery, SNAP seemed more specialised for remote sensing tasks, and R appeared useful for statistical comparison and reproducible analysis. This comparison made me reflect on how software choice can shape the way analysis is carried out.

Another part of the practical that stood out to me was the introduction to spectral signatures and basic image statistics. Although this week was still introductory, it showed that remote sensing can move beyond visual interpretation and towards quantitative comparison of land cover types. This seems particularly relevant for urban and environmental applications, where distinguishing features such as vegetation, water, bare earth, and built-up land is often important.

The practical was relatively introductory, but it was useful in showing that remote sensing analysis begins with sourcing data and understanding image products, rather than immediately producing final outputs.I also found it useful that the practical introduced workflow management through GitHub and .gitignore. At first this seemed like a small technical detail, but it made me realise that handling large raster datasets requires organisation as well as analysis. This is clearly important for the portfolio and online learning diary, especially since the module expects regular weekly updates.

Overall, Week 1 gave me a much more structured understanding of remote sensing. I now see it not only as a way of viewing the Earth from above, but as a full analytical workflow involving data sourcing, processing, software choice, and interpretation.
